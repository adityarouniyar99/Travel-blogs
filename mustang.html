Beautiful Places Around the Mustang

Explore some of the most stunning destinations in Mustang

Muktinath Temple:

High in the rugged folds of Mustang, where the air thins and the silence deepens, Muktinath Temple rises like a whisper of peace against the vast Himalayan sky. Standing at an altitude of 3,710 meters, this sacred sanctuary is more than just a place of worship — it is a destination of healing. Travelers, pilgrims, wanderers, and dream-seekers from across the world walk this path not simply to reach a temple, but to rediscover something gentle within themselves.


The landscape here feels ancient. The mountains don’t just surround you — they hold you. Every gust of wind carries a story older than memory, older than maps. The journey itself becomes part of the experience. Whether you trek along the winding trails from Jomsom or take a jeep ride through the valleys carved by rivers and time, the road to Muktinath feels like stepping slowly out of the noise of the world and into the clarity of your own breath.Muktinath holds a unique spiritual harmony, honored by both Hindu and Buddhist traditions. For Hindus, it is the sacred place of liberation — Mukti, freedom for the soul. For Buddhists, it is home to the eternal flame of Jwala Mai, a symbol of life, balance, and the ever-present dance of natural energy. Inside the temple compound, the eternal flame flickers quietly next to running water — a rare and beautiful meeting of elements that feels almost otherworldly.

Lo Manthang:


Deep within the desert-like valleys of Upper Mustang, where the winds carve stories into cliff faces and the horizon looks limitless, lies Lo Manthang — a timeless walled kingdom resting under the watchful silence of the Himalayas. At first sight, it feels like something from another century — and honestly, in many ways, it is. This is the last whisper of ancient Tibet in Nepal, preserved in dust, stone, and sacred prayer. Arriving in Lo Manthang feels like stepping into a memory you've never lived before. The landscape is raw and dramatic — wide open skies, ochre-colored hills, and an endless stillness that makes your thoughts echo inside your chest. The air here feels different. A little thinner, a little older. Like it’s carrying centuries of untold stories. Travelers don’t just reach Lo Manthang — they slowly enter it, like crossing a threshold into another world.


Every year, in the hidden walled kingdom of Lo Manthang, the Tiji Festival unfolds like a sacred play whispered straight out of history. It’s a three-day celebration of good overcoming evil, rooted in Tibetan Buddhist tradition and passed down through centuries of storytelling. Think vibrant colors, echoing horns, slow rhythmic chants, and masked dances that make the air feel charged with mystery and meaning. The festival tells the legend of Dorje Shunu, a deity believed to have saved the region from destruction by defeating a malevolent demon. But this isn’t just a story you hear — it’s something you see, feel, and breathe as monks reenact it through dramatic and mesmerizing dance performances in front of the royal palace courtyard.

Marpha


Tucked gently between the towering Himalayan ridges of Lower Mustang, Marpha arrives like a breath of calm after a long journey. If Mustang is dramatic and wild, Marpha is her soft-spoken, warm-hearted side — a village where time slows down just enough for you to remember how to breathe again. Whitewashed houses, flat rooftops, fluttering prayer flags, and narrow stone lanes create a scene that feels almost unreal, like stepping into a living postcard.Marpha is famously known as the “Apple Capital of Nepal,” and oh, it lives up to the title. The orchards stretch across gentle terraces, glowing golden and green under the afternoon sun. Here, apples aren’t just fruit — they’re part of the village’s identity. Fresh apples, warm apple pie, apple cider, apple brandy — every taste carries the sweetness of the land and the tenderness of the people who care for it. It’s comfort, woven into flavor.


Walking through Marpha feels like wandering inside a secret kept safe for centuries. The village is quiet, but not empty — it’s full of soft footfalls, laughter floating out of doorways, and the scent of home-cooked meals drifting in the air. The buildings are built close together, their stones smooth from years of footsteps. Every corner looks like a photograph waiting to be taken, and every step feels like you’re being gently guided by the mountains themselves. There’s a certain peace here — the kind that doesn’t announce itself loudly, but settles around you like a warm shawl. The nearby Thakali culture adds layers of hospitality, tradition, and warmth to the place. You’ll meet people who greet you with smiles that feel like sunlight, and conversations that stay with you longer than expected. Sit for a meal in a traditional Thakali kitchen — the flavors, the rhythm, the care — it all feels like a quiet embrace.Not far from the main village, the Dhumba Lake rests like a slice of sky fallen to the earth — still, reflective, and deeply calming. A short walk leads you there, and when the lake meets the mountains in its reflection, it’s hard not to feel something shift inside your chest.
